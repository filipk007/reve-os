# Competitive Framing

How to win competitive deals without naming, bashing, or acknowledging
competitors directly. The goal: shift the evaluation criteria so you're
playing a game only you can win.

## Core Rule

Never name a competitor unless the prospect brings them up first. Even
then, acknowledge briefly and pivot to your frame.

## Technique 1: Category Reframing

Redefine the category so the prospect evaluates you differently than they
evaluate incumbents.

**Example:** Instead of "We're a better enrichment tool," try "Most teams
treat enrichment as a data step. We treat it as a GTM intelligence layer
that informs every outbound motion."

You're not competing in their category. You're creating yours.

## Technique 2: Dimension Shifting

Every product wins on some dimension. Find yours and make it the dimension
that matters most.

**How to apply:**
1. List dimensions: speed, accuracy, depth, ease of use, cost, integration,
   flexibility, support, time-to-value
2. Identify 1-2 where you're clearly strongest
3. Build messaging that elevates those dimensions as the real buying criteria

**Example:** "The question isn't which tool has the most data points — it's
which one actually helps your reps send better emails in less time."

## Technique 3: The "And" vs "Or" Frame

Position competitors as forcing tradeoffs that you eliminate.

**Pattern:** "Most tools make you choose between X and Y. We built for both."

**Example:** "You shouldn't have to choose between personalization quality
and volume. Our approach gives you both without the tradeoff."

## Technique 4: Future-Pacing the Architecture

Make the prospect imagine where their current approach breaks in 12 months.

**Pattern:** "That works at your current scale. What happens when you 3x
the team?"

**Example:** "The real question isn't what works today — it's what still
works when you're running 10 campaigns across 4 segments with 3 different
ICPs."

This plants doubt about the competitor's scalability without naming them.

## When a Prospect Names a Competitor

1. **Acknowledge:** "Yeah, they're solid. A lot of teams start there."
2. **Pivot:** "The thing we hear from teams who've tried that approach is..."
3. **Reframe:** Shift to a dimension or criteria they haven't considered

Never say: "They're bad at X" or "We're better than them at Y."
Instead: "The question we'd ask is whether X matters more than Y for
where you're headed."

## Anti-Patterns
- Trash-talking competitors (makes you look insecure)
- Feature-by-feature comparison charts (commoditizes you)
- Saying "we're the only ones who..." (rarely true, easily disproven)
- Getting defensive when a competitor is mentioned
- Over-explaining why competitors are wrong

## When to Use Each Technique

| Situation | Technique |
|-----------|-----------|
| Prospect hasn't evaluated anyone yet | Category Reframing |
| Prospect is comparing you to a specific tool | Dimension Shifting |
| Prospect sees all tools as equivalent | "And" vs "Or" Frame |
| Prospect is committed to current stack | Future-Pacing |
