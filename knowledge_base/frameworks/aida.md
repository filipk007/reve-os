# AIDA Framework

Attention, Interest, Desire, Action. A four-stage framework for
longer-form content where you need to build momentum from initial
hook to final conversion.

## When to Use AIDA

- Campaign briefs that need full narrative structure
- Landing pages and product marketing copy
- Longer emails (nurture sequences, post-demo follow-ups)
- Content marketing: blog posts, guides, case study narratives
- Sales decks and presentation scripts

## When NOT to Use AIDA

- Cold outbound emails (too long — use PVC or PAS instead)
- LinkedIn connection notes or DMs (too structured)
- Quick follow-ups or transactional messages
- Any context where brevity beats narrative

## The Four Stages

### A — Attention (Hook)

Stop the scroll. Break the pattern. You have 2-3 seconds.
**Techniques:** Contrarian statement, specific statistic, question that
reveals a gap, bold claim with proof preview.
**Rules:** Relevant > clever, specific beats clever, no clickbait,
match tone to channel.

**Examples:**
- "73% of outbound emails get deleted unread. The other 27% have one
  thing in common."
- "Your SDR team isn't underperforming. Your targeting is."

### I — Interest (Relevance)

Earn the right to keep attention. Connect the hook to something they
personally care about. Stay in THEIR world.
**Techniques:** Describe their situation, share a trend, ask a resonant
question, mirror their language.
**Rules:** Bridge from hook to their context, personalization matters
most here, don't talk about yourself yet.

**Example:** "If you're scaling a B2B outbound team right now, you're
probably stuck in the same loop: hire more reps, send more emails,
watch reply rates drop. The math stops working around rep #8."

### D — Desire (Proof + Aspiration)

Create desire for the OUTCOME, not your product. Use proof to make it
feel achievable. Case studies, social proof, and specific results live here.
**Techniques:** Case study, social proof, specific metrics, before/after
contrast, testimonial.
**Rules:** Proof must be specific, match to their situation, 1-2 strong
proof points beat 5 weak ones.

**Example:** "[Similar company] — 6 AEs, Series B, reply rates falling.
In 90 days: 8% to 18% reply rates, $2.3M added to pipeline, zero new SDRs."

### A — Action (CTA)

One clear action, low friction. Should feel like the natural next step.
**Techniques:** Question CTA ("Worth exploring?"), Specific CTA ("Grab
15 minutes"), Value CTA ("I'll send the breakdown"), Choice CTA ("Reply
A or B").
**Rules:** ONE action not three, match friction to relationship, don't
undermine desire with a hard sell.

## AIDA by Format

| Format | Attention | Interest | Desire | Action |
|--------|-----------|----------|--------|--------|
| Landing page | Headline + subhead | Problem section | Proof section | CTA button |
| Campaign brief | Hook paragraph | ICP context | Case study/results | Sequence CTA |
| Nurture email | Subject + first line | Pain/relevance | Proof point | Soft CTA |
| Sales deck | Opening slide | Their world | Customer story | Next steps |
| Blog post | Title + opening | Problem deep-dive | Solution + proof | CTA/subscribe |

## AIDA vs Other Frameworks

| | AIDA | PAS | BAB | PVC |
|---|------|-----|-----|-----|
| Length | Long | Short | Medium | Short |
| Stages | 4 | 3 | 3 | 3 |
| Best for | Content, briefs | Cold email | Product messaging | Cold email |
| Emotional arc | Curiosity to action | Pain to relief | Gap to bridge | Respect to ask |

## Anti-Patterns
- Spending too long on Attention and losing them before Interest
- Interest section that talks about you instead of them
- Desire section without specific proof (just claims)
- Multiple CTAs competing for attention
- Using AIDA for short-form where PVC or PAS works better
- Clickbait hooks that the content doesn't deliver on
