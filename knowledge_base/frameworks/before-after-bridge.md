# Before-After-Bridge (BAB) Framework

Paint the current state (before). Describe the desired state (after). Show
how your product is the bridge. BAB works because it creates a vision gap
— the distance between where they are and where they want to be — and
positions you as the path to close it.

## When to Use BAB

- Product-led messaging where the transformation is clear
- Campaign briefs that need narrative structure
- Longer-form content: landing pages, case studies, longer emails
- When the prospect knows their current state is suboptimal but hasn't
  articulated what "better" looks like

## When NOT to Use BAB

- Ultra-short cold emails (PVC or PAS are tighter)
- When you don't know their current state well enough
- When the "before" isn't painful enough to motivate change
- Transactional or feature-comparison contexts

## The Three Components

### Before (Current State)

Describe where the prospect is right now. Be specific and relatable.
They should read this and think: "That's exactly my situation."

**Rules:**
- Be descriptive, not judgmental
- Use concrete details, not abstract pain
- Mirror their language — how THEY describe their situation
- Don't exaggerate — if it doesn't ring true, they check out

**Good:** "Right now, your team spends Monday mornings building prospect
lists by hand. By Tuesday, half those leads are already in someone else's
pipeline. By Friday, you're wondering why reply rates keep dropping."

**Bad:** "Your outbound is broken and your team is wasting time." (too
blunt, no narrative, feels like an attack)

### After (Desired State)

Describe what their world looks like when the problem is solved. Make it
specific, achievable, and desirable. This isn't fantasy — it's a credible
future state.

**Rules:**
- Be specific enough to feel real (not "everything is amazing")
- Include concrete outcomes: metrics, time savings, results
- Make it achievable — overpromising kills credibility
- Connect to what they actually care about (outcomes, not features)

**Good:** "Your reps start each day with a prioritized list of prospects
who match your ICP, enriched with the context they need to write a
relevant first line in 30 seconds. Reply rates double. Pipeline becomes
predictable."

**Bad:** "Imagine a world where everything just works and revenue
flows effortlessly." (too vague, sounds like a fantasy)

### Bridge (Your Product/Solution)

Show how you get them from before to after. This is where your product
enters — but as a bridge, not a feature list.

**Rules:**
- The bridge should feel inevitable after the before/after contrast
- One to two sentences max — don't over-explain
- Focus on HOW the transformation happens, not what features you have
- Include a CTA that invites them to see the bridge in action

**Good:** "That's what [product] does. We turn your ICP definition
into a daily pipeline of research-ready prospects, so your team sells
instead of searches. Want to see how it works for [their company type]?"

**Bad:** "Our platform offers AI-powered lead scoring, 47 data
integrations, real-time enrichment, and a Chrome extension." (feature
dump — they don't care about the bridge's engineering, just that it
gets them across)

## Full BAB Example (Campaign Brief)

> **Before:** Your SDRs spend 40% of their week researching prospects
> and writing personalized emails from scratch. They handle maybe
> 30 accounts each, and half of those aren't even a fit.
>
> **After:** Each rep covers 150 accounts with emails that reference
> real company signals — funding rounds, executive hires, product
> launches. Reply rates hit 12%. Pipeline is consistent week over week.
>
> **Bridge:** [Product] automates the research and personalization
> layer so your team focuses on what they're actually good at:
> having conversations that close deals.

## BAB for Different Formats

| Format | Before Length | After Length | Bridge Length |
|--------|-------------|-------------|--------------|
| Cold email | 1-2 sentences | 1-2 sentences | 1 sentence + CTA |
| LinkedIn DM | 1 sentence | 1 sentence | 1 sentence |
| Landing page | Full paragraph | Full paragraph | Section with proof |
| Campaign brief | Detailed scenario | Detailed outcome | Product + CTA |
| Case study | Client's old reality | Client's results | How you got them there |

## BAB vs PAS

| | BAB | PAS |
|---|-----|-----|
| Emotional driver | Aspiration (want the "after") | Pain (escape the "before") |
| Best for | Product-led, vision-driven | Pain-driven, urgency-driven |
| Tone | Optimistic, forward-looking | Empathetic, cost-of-inaction |
| Length | Medium to long | Short to medium |
| Risk | "After" feels unrealistic | "Pain" feels presumptuous |

## Anti-Patterns
- "Before" that doesn't match their actual situation
- "After" that sounds too good to be true
- Bridge that's just a feature list
- Skipping the "before" and going straight to features
- Making the "before" feel insulting rather than empathetic
