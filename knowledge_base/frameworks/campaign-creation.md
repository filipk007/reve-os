# Campaign Creation Framework

Campaigns fail because people start with messages instead of context. The best
outbound doesn't come from better copy — it comes from deeper knowledge about
the prospect's world than anyone else has. This framework inverts the typical
approach: context first, strategy second, messages last.

## When to Use

- Before building any new outbound campaign
- When entering a new segment or vertical
- When existing campaigns underperform (reply rates below 3%)
- When you have access to deep, segment-specific data sources
- Before scaling — prove the play works on 50 before running 500

## When NOT to Use

- Quick one-off follow-ups to warm leads
- Warm introductions where you already have context
- Nurture sequences to existing pipeline
- Replicating a proven campaign to a new batch of the same segment

## Core Principle — Context Before Copy

The quality of your outbound is a direct function of the context loaded before
writing anything. Not the prompt. Not the framework. The context.

The goal is a **Permissionless Value Prop (PVP)**: a message so valuable the
prospect would pay to receive it. You're delivering market intelligence, not
a pitch. The meeting is the natural next step, not the ask.

PVP is the V in [[pvc]] taken to its extreme. Instead of one sentence
of value, you're leading with an insight that makes the prospect think: "How do
they know this about my market?"

## The Five Steps

### Step 1 — Invert from Best Deals

Start from closed-won, not ICP theory. Download call recordings. Analyze
transcripts. Compare closed-won against closed-lost. Find what actually made
people convert — not what you think made them convert.

Then ask the harder question: what public data could have predicted this?

**Rules:**
- Look at your last 20 closed-won deals, not a theoretical ICP doc
- Identify the signals that appeared before they entered pipeline
  (see [[signal-taxonomy]] for signal types)
- Find the common thread — what pain did they all share?
- Work backward from conversion to discover targeting criteria

### Step 2 — Find Discoverable Pain

Standard enrichment signals — job changes, funding rounds, LinkedIn activity —
are table stakes. Everyone has them. Your competitors are writing the same email
to the same person with the same data.

The teams pulling ahead find data sources unique to the segment.

**Rules:**
- Regulated industries are a treasure trove: permits, filings, compliance data,
  licensing records, court documents
- Job postings reveal priorities better than any intent signal
- Tech stack changes signal active workflow re-evaluation
- Industry associations, public databases, and government records are goldmines
  most enrichment vendors don't package
- **Drill, not peanut butter.** Go deep on one segment where you have rich data
  and a strong value prop. Don't spread thin across ten segments with surface-level
  context. Three similar lines of data on 50 accounts beats one line on 500.

### Step 3 — Build the Context Folder

Before writing a single message, assemble everything the AI (or human) needs
to reason like your best rep:

- **ICP documentation** — who you're targeting and why
- **Call transcripts** — real language from won and lost deals
- **Competitive intelligence** — [[displacement-playbook]] and positioning
- **Objection patterns** — what they'll push back on
- **Product data** — what you actually do, in their language
- **Data source access** — the proprietary data from Step 2
- **Persona context** — how this buyer thinks (CRO vs CTO vs founder)
- **Industry context** — what's happening in their vertical right now

The context compounds. The first campaign is the heavy lift. Every campaign after
builds on what you've already loaded.

### Step 4 — Plan the Play

Strategy before execution. Answer three questions before writing anything:

1. **What unique data do we have?** (from Step 2)
2. **What pain does it prove?** (from Step 1)
3. **What's the PVP — what would they pay to know?**

Then specify:

- **Segment:** Who specifically? (vertical + company size + signal)
- **Data edge:** What do we know that competitors don't?
- **Pain hypothesis:** What pain does the data prove?
- **PVP:** What would the prospect pay to know?
- **Persona:** Who are we writing to? (match to persona doc)
- **Framework:** [[pvc]], [[pain-agitate-solve]], or [[before-after-bridge]]?
- **Sequence:** Which cadence? (see [[cold-email-sequence]])
- **Signals:** Which triggers open the conversation? (see [[signal-taxonomy]])

### Step 5 — Execute Narrow, Then Scale

Start with 30-50 prospects. Not 500. Not 5,000.

**Rules:**
- Run the first batch and measure: reply rate, positive reply rate, meeting
  conversion
- A working campaign: 5%+ reply rate, 2%+ positive reply rate
- If it's working, expand to 100-200. Then scale.
- If it's not working, revisit Step 2 (wrong data) or Step 4 (wrong angle)
- Scaling a bad campaign just produces bad results faster
- "Pointing a thousand cars off a cliff" — prove the road goes somewhere first

## The PVP Test

One litmus test for every message in your campaign: **"Would the prospect pay
to receive this?"**

If yes, you have a PVP. If no, you have a pitch.

**Good:** "154 roofing contractors in your region used your product in 10%+ of
their permitted jobs but give 60-90% of their business to competitors. Here are
the top five — with revenue, reviews, total permits, and exact brand mix."

**Bad:** "I noticed your company is a leader in the roofing industry. I'd love
to show you how we help companies like yours grow their contractor network."

The good version delivers market intelligence the recipient can use regardless
of whether they take a meeting. The bad version is a pitch dressed as research.

Nobody ever called market intelligence "creepy." Generic personalization gets
that label daily.

## Anti-Patterns

- **"Congrats on your new role" personalization.** Lazy. No value. Everyone
  sends it. (See [[signal-taxonomy]] — connect the signal to their pain.)
- **Spray and pray.** 500 emails before proving the play works on 50. You're
  not scaling outbound — you're scaling noise.
- **Starting with the message.** If you're writing copy before loading context,
  you're doing it backward.
- **Standard signals only.** Job changes, funding, LinkedIn activity — everyone
  has these. They're baseline, not edge.
- **Peanut-buttering across segments.** Thin context across many segments beats
  deep context on one? Never. Go deep.
- **Skipping the inversion.** Guessing at ICP instead of analyzing closed-won
  deals. Your data knows more than your assumptions.
- **Scaling before proving.** If 50 prospects didn't respond, 5,000 won't either.
  Fix the play first.
- **Feature pitches disguised as insights.** If the "value" in your PVP is just
  your product description, it's not a PVP.
