# Common Cold Outbound Objections

## Core Principle

Every objection is a signal, not a rejection. The prospect is telling you what they need to hear before they can engage. Your job is to reduce friction, not overcome resistance.

## Response Framework: ARC

- **A**cknowledge — validate what they said without agreeing to end the conversation
- **R**eframe — shift the lens to something they haven't considered
- **C**uriosity — ask one question that earns the next 30 seconds

---

## 1. "Not interested"

- **Underlying concern**: You haven't earned relevance yet. They're pattern-matching you to every other cold caller.
- **Strategy**: Relevance Interrupt
- **Pattern**: Acknowledge the cold context. Name something specific to their world that signals you did homework. Ask if that topic is on their radar.
- **Redirect**: "Totally fair — I called out of the blue. Quick question before I let you go: is [specific pain point relevant to their role/industry] something your team is dealing with right now?"

## 2. "We already have a solution"

- **Underlying concern**: Switching cost feels high. They assume you want them to rip and replace.
- **Strategy**: Coexistence Frame
- **Pattern**: Acknowledge their current solution positively. Position your value as additive or complementary, not competitive. Ask about a gap their current tool might not cover.
- **Redirect**: "That makes sense — most companies in [industry] do. Out of curiosity, how are you currently handling [specific gap or adjacent problem]?"

## 3. "Send me some info"

- **Underlying concern**: Polite dismissal, or genuine interest with no time right now. Treat it as the latter.
- **Strategy**: Qualify the Send
- **Pattern**: Agree to send info. Ask one qualifying question so you can send the right thing. Lock a follow-up touchpoint.
- **Redirect**: "Happy to — I want to send you the right thing though. Are you more focused on [outcome A] or [outcome B] right now? I'll tailor it and follow up [specific day]."

## 4. "Too expensive" / "What's the price?"

- **Underlying concern**: They're anchoring on cost before understanding value. Or they have a real budget constraint.
- **Strategy**: Value Before Price
- **Pattern**: Defer exact pricing to a conversation where you can tie it to their specific ROI. Acknowledge budget matters. Reframe to cost of the problem, not cost of the solution.
- **Redirect**: "Pricing depends on scope, so I want to make sure I give you an accurate picture. Can I ask — what is [problem] costing you right now in terms of [time/revenue/headcount]?"

## 5. "It's not a priority right now"

- **Underlying concern**: Genuine — they have competing priorities. Or they don't yet see this as urgent.
- **Strategy**: Priority Ladder
- **Pattern**: Accept their priority stack. Ask what is the priority. Find a way to tie your value to what they're already focused on.
- **Redirect**: "That's fair — what's the biggest thing on your plate this quarter? Reason I ask is [brief tie to how your solution supports that priority]."

## 6. "Who is this?" / "How did you get my number?"

- **Underlying concern**: Guard is up. They feel intruded upon.
- **Strategy**: Transparent Disarm
- **Pattern**: Introduce yourself clearly and briefly. Be honest about how you found them. Earn 15 seconds with a sharp reason for calling.
- **Redirect**: "Fair question — I'm [name] from [company]. I found you on [source]. I'm reaching out because [one sentence about why specifically them]. Does that resonate at all, or am I off base?"

## 7. "We're locked into a contract"

- **Underlying concern**: They literally cannot switch right now, or they believe they can't.
- **Strategy**: Calendar Bookmark
- **Pattern**: Accept the timeline constraint. Ask when the contract is up. Offer to reconnect at a useful time so they can evaluate options before renewal.
- **Redirect**: "Totally understand — when does that come up for renewal? I'd love to connect [timeframe before renewal] so you have options on the table before you auto-renew."

## 8. "I need to talk to my team"

- **Underlying concern**: They may not be the decision-maker, or they need consensus. Could also be a stall.
- **Strategy**: Enable the Champion
- **Pattern**: Support their internal process. Offer to give them something shareable. Ask who else would need to weigh in and whether a brief group call would be easier.
- **Redirect**: "Makes total sense — would it help if I put together a one-pager your team can review? And who else would typically weigh in on something like this?"

## 9. "Call me next quarter"

- **Underlying concern**: Not urgent enough to act now. May also be a brush-off.
- **Strategy**: Seed and Schedule
- **Pattern**: Accept the timeline. Plant a reason why starting the conversation now (not buying — just evaluating) saves time later. Lock a specific date.
- **Redirect**: "Will do. Most folks I talk to like to start evaluating [timeframe] before they need to move — would the first week of [month] work for a 15-minute intro so you're not scrambling later?"

## 10. "Remove me from your list"

- **Underlying concern**: They are done. Respect it immediately.
- **Strategy**: Graceful Exit
- **Pattern**: Confirm removal. No tricks, no last pitch. Leave the door open with dignity.
- **Redirect**: "Done — I'll remove you right now. If anything changes down the road, I'm easy to find. Appreciate your time."

---

## Usage Rules

1. Never stack multiple objection responses — handle one, then listen.
2. Silence after your redirect question is good. Let them answer.
3. If you get the same objection twice, accept it and exit gracefully.
4. Tone matters more than words. Calm, curious, unhurried.
5. These are patterns, not scripts. Adapt language to match the prospect's communication style.
