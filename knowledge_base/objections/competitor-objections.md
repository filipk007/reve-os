# Competitor Objections

## Core Principle

When a prospect says "We already use X," they are telling you they've already solved this problem in their mind. Your job is not to unsell their current solution — it's to open a window they didn't know was closed.

## The ACC Pattern: Acknowledge-Contrast-Curiosity

### Step 1: Acknowledge

Validate their choice. They spent time evaluating, got buy-in, implemented, and built workflows. Dismissing that work insults their judgment.

**Tone**: Genuine respect. Not "that's great, but..." — actually mean it.

**Structure**: "[Competitor] is solid for [what they're known for]. Smart choice."

### Step 2: Contrast

Introduce a dimension, use case, or outcome they may not have optimized for. This is not a feature comparison. It's a lens shift.

**Rules for contrasting**:
- Pick ONE dimension only — do not list five advantages
- Choose a dimension where the gap is real and provable
- Frame it as a category difference, not a product flaw
- Use language like "where teams tend to hit a ceiling" or "the piece that usually gets handled manually"

**Structure**: "Where we tend to come in is [specific dimension] — it's the part that [competitor] wasn't really built to handle because they focused on [their strength]."

### Step 3: Curiosity

Ask a single question that makes them reflect on whether that dimension matters. The question should be answerable without committing to anything.

**Structure**: "Is [that dimension] something your team runs into, or have you found a workaround?"

## Scenario Patterns

### A. Happy With Competitor

The prospect is genuinely satisfied. They're not looking.

- **Signal**: "We love [competitor]," "It's working great," "No complaints"
- **Acknowledge**: "Good — that's what I hear from a lot of teams using [competitor]. They do [core strength] really well."
- **Contrast**: Introduce an adjacent problem or future-state challenge. Not what the competitor does poorly today, but what the prospect will need tomorrow.
- **Curiosity**: "As you scale [specific growth vector — headcount, deal volume, markets], how are you thinking about [the dimension that strains at scale]?"
- **Exit rule**: If they confirm they're happy and the contrast doesn't land, thank them and move on. Do not push. Offer to check back in 6 months.

### B. Evaluating Competitors

The prospect is actively comparing options. You're entering a live evaluation.

- **Signal**: "We're looking at a few options," "We have a shortlist," "We're in an eval"
- **Acknowledge**: "That's a smart move — better to evaluate now than get locked into something that doesn't fit."
- **Contrast**: Position on the evaluation criteria they might be under-weighting. Most evaluations over-index on features and under-index on implementation speed, ongoing support, or adaptability.
- **Curiosity**: "What's the #1 thing that will make or break this decision for your team — is it [common criterion] or something else?"
- **Goal**: Get added to the evaluation. Offer a side-by-side in their specific use case, not a generic demo.

### C. Just Bought Competitor

They signed recently. Switching cost is at maximum.

- **Signal**: "We just implemented [competitor]," "We signed last month," "We're still onboarding"
- **Acknowledge**: "Got it — you're in the middle of rolling it out, so the last thing you need is another vendor conversation."
- **Contrast**: Do not contrast now. Instead, plant a seed for the renewal window.
- **Curiosity**: "Totally respect the timing. When does your contract come up for review? I'd love to connect [30-60 days before renewal] so you can benchmark where things stand."
- **Action**: Log the renewal date. Set a reminder. Send one value-add touchpoint (industry insight, benchmark data) at the midpoint — no pitch, just relevance.

### D. Competitor Is Free / Cheap

The prospect chose on price. They may be getting what they pay for, or the free tier genuinely meets their needs.

- **Signal**: "We use the free version," "It's cheap enough," "We don't have budget for this"
- **Acknowledge**: "Makes sense — if [competitor's free tier] covers what you need, there's no reason to spend more."
- **Contrast**: Free tools typically lack depth in one of three areas: scale, support, or customization. Identify which one matters most for their use case.
- **Curiosity**: "Where does [free tool] start to feel limiting — is it when [volume threshold], when you need [specific capability], or has it been smooth so far?"
- **Exit rule**: If the free tool genuinely works, respect that. Not every prospect is a fit. Move on.

## Hard Rules

1. **Never bash the competitor.** Not even subtly. "They're good but..." is still bashing. Just say they're good.
2. **Never claim feature parity + more.** It's rarely true and never believable.
3. **Never ask "Are you happy with [competitor]?"** — it's a trap question that puts them on the defensive. Assume they're happy and contrast from there.
4. **One contrast per conversation.** Multiple contrasts sound like a feature dump. Pick the sharpest one.
5. **If they say no twice, stop.** Two clear rejections means you exit with grace, not a third attempt.
6. **Log competitive intel.** Every competitor mention is data. Track which competitors appear most, which contrast points land, and which scenarios convert.

## Contrast Dimension Examples

When choosing your contrast dimension, consider these categories:

| Category | Example Contrast |
|----------|-----------------|
| Scale | "Works great up to 50 users, but teams above 200 usually hit [ceiling]" |
| Workflow | "Handles [process A] well, but [process B] usually lives in a spreadsheet alongside it" |
| Insight | "Great for execution, but the reporting on [metric] tends to require manual pulls" |
| Speed | "Solid product, but implementation typically takes [X months] vs. [your timeframe]" |
| Support | "Good self-serve, but when you need [specialized help], response time becomes a factor" |
| Integration | "Native for [ecosystem A], but connecting to [ecosystem B] usually requires middleware" |

Choose the dimension where you have the strongest proof points — customer stories, benchmarks, or demo-able differences.
