# Timing Objections

## Core Principle

Timing objections are the most common and the most honest. The prospect is rarely lying — they genuinely have other priorities. Your job is not to convince them that now is the right time. Your job is to lock a future touchpoint so you stay in the conversation when the timing does shift.

## The Calendar Move Technique

Three steps, every time:

### Step 1: Accept the Timeline

Do not fight the timing. Do not argue that "there's never a perfect time." Agree with their reality. This earns trust and separates you from every rep who pushes through timing objections.

**Structure**: "That makes sense — [restate their situation briefly]."

### Step 2: Plant a Seed

Share one insight, data point, or observation that creates a reason to think about this before the timing feels right. The seed is not a pitch — it's a thought that stays with them.

**Rules for seed planting**:
- Keep it to one sentence
- Make it specific to their situation, not generic
- Frame it as something others in their position have experienced
- Do not attach urgency — let the insight create its own pull

**Structure**: "One thing I've seen with teams in [similar situation] is that [insight about what happens when they wait]. Not saying that's you — just worth having on your radar."

### Step 3: Lock a Future Touchpoint

Propose a specific date or timeframe for reconnecting. Vague follow-ups die. Specific calendar holds survive.

**Rules for locking**:
- Suggest a specific week, not "sometime next quarter"
- Frame the follow-up as low-commitment: 15 minutes, no prep needed
- Give them a reason for that specific timing: "That's usually when teams start planning for [next cycle]"
- Confirm via email with a calendar invite immediately after the call

**Structure**: "Would the first week of [month] work for a 15-minute check-in? That way you'll have options ready before [relevant deadline or cycle]."

## Scenario Patterns

### A. Budget Cycle Timing

The prospect cannot spend money right now. Budget is allocated, frozen, or under review.

- **Signal**: "Our budget is set for this year," "We're in a freeze," "Budget planning is in [month]"
- **Accept**: "Totally get it — budget cycles are budget cycles."
- **Seed**: "Most of the teams I work with start the evaluation 60-90 days before budget opens so they can walk into planning with a clear business case and numbers ready. Saves a lot of scrambling."
- **Lock**: "When does your next planning cycle kick off? I'd love to connect [6-8 weeks before that] so you have everything you need if this makes the cut."
- **Between now and then**: Send one piece of value (ROI calculator, benchmark report, case study from their industry) at the midpoint. No pitch — just ammunition they can use in their budget conversation.

### B. Too Busy Right Now

The prospect has too much on their plate. They cannot give this attention.

- **Signal**: "Slammed right now," "Maybe after [event/launch/quarter-end]," "I can't take on anything new"
- **Accept**: "I hear you — the last thing I want to do is add to your plate."
- **Seed**: "Quick thought: a lot of the folks I talk to in [role] end up looking at this after [their busy period] and wish they'd started the research phase earlier so they weren't evaluating under pressure. Just something to consider."
- **Lock**: "How about I reach out the week of [date after their busy period]? No prep on your end — just a 15-minute intro so you can see if it's even worth a deeper look."
- **Calibration**: If they're genuinely drowning, do not push the seed hard. Sometimes the best move is: "I'll send you a one-liner email and you can flag it for later. No follow-up calls until you say so."

### C. Just Implemented Something

They recently deployed a new tool or process. They are in adoption mode.

- **Signal**: "We just rolled out [system]," "We're 3 months into a new [process]," "We made a big change recently"
- **Accept**: "That's a big lift — makes sense to let that settle before looking at anything else."
- **Seed**: "What I typically see is that teams know within 90-120 days whether the new system is covering all their use cases or if there are gaps. That's usually when the conversation becomes useful."
- **Lock**: "Mind if I check in around [4-5 months from implementation]? By then you'll have real data on what's working and what's not, and I can share how other teams have approached [the likely gap]."
- **Long game**: This is a 6-12 month nurture. Send relevant content at months 3, 6, and 9. The renewal window is your real opportunity.

### D. Organizational Change in Progress

The prospect's company is going through a merger, reorg, leadership change, or strategic pivot.

- **Signal**: "We're going through a reorg," "New leadership just came in," "We're being acquired," "Strategy is shifting"
- **Accept**: "That's a lot of moving pieces — I'd imagine the last thing anyone needs is a vendor pitch in the middle of that."
- **Seed**: "One pattern I've noticed: after a reorg, new leadership usually wants to see quick wins in [relevant area] within their first 90 days. Having an option ready to go can position your team well."
- **Lock**: "Would it make sense to connect once the dust settles — maybe [realistic timeframe based on their situation]? Even just to see if priorities have shifted in a direction where this becomes relevant."
- **Sensitivity**: Organizational change creates anxiety. Be especially respectful of their time and headspace. Do not try to pitch to the new leadership over their head.

## Follow-Up Execution

Locking the touchpoint is only half the battle. The follow-up must be executed precisely.

### Immediately after the call
- Send a confirmation email: "As discussed, I'll reach out the week of [date]. In the meantime, here's [one relevant resource]."
- Add a calendar reminder for yourself with context notes

### At the midpoint
- Send one value-add touchpoint. No pitch. Options:
  - Industry benchmark or trend report
  - Case study from a similar company
  - Relevant article with a one-line note: "Saw this and thought of your [situation]. No response needed."

### At the follow-up date
- Reference the original conversation specifically
- Acknowledge that time has passed and things may have changed
- Ask an open question, not a pitch: "Last time we spoke, you were [situation]. How did that play out?"

### If they don't respond to the follow-up
- One more attempt 5-7 days later with a shorter, lighter touch
- After two no-responses, move to a quarterly nurture cadence
- Never guilt-trip about the missed follow-up

## Hard Rules

1. **Never say "there's never a perfect time."** It's dismissive and they've heard it from every rep.
2. **Never manufacture false urgency.** "This offer expires" or "We only have 3 spots" destroys trust in cold outbound.
3. **Always get a specific timeframe.** "Next quarter" becomes "first week of April." Vague kills deals.
4. **One seed per conversation.** Multiple seeds feel like a pitch disguised as patience.
5. **Respect "not now" that means "not ever."** If the same prospect gives you a timing objection three times across three touchpoints, they are not a timing objection — they are a no. Move on.
6. **Log everything.** The follow-up date, the context, the seed you planted, their situation. Your future self needs this to have a relevant second conversation.
